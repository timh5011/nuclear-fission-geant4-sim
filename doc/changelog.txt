⏺ Two separate things bundled into that warning — let me unpack them.                                                                                                                                   
                                                                                                                                                                                                      
  1. What "critical mass" means here
                                                                                                                                                                                                        
  A bare sphere of pure ²³⁵U at 19.1 g/cm³ goes critical (k_eff = 1, self-sustaining chain reaction) at about 47–52 kg, which is a sphere of radius ~8.6 cm or a cube ~17 cm on a side. Above that,     
  every fission produces on average more than one fission in the next generation → exponential growth. Below it, neutrons leak out faster than they can cause new fissions. (For reference: Little Boy  
  used ~64 kg of HEU.)                                                                                                                                                                                  
                  
  Your current world is a 20 cm cube (10 cm half-side), so you couldn't even fit a critical mass in the world volume — but you could get uncomfortably close to it (e.g. a 5 cm cube of pure ²³⁵U is    
  ~2.4 kg, well below critical, but a 10 cm cube is ~19 kg, getting noticeable neutron multiplication even though still subcritical).
                                                                                                                                                                                                        
  2. Why this matters for Geant4 specifically                                                                                                                                                           
   
  Geant4's HP package is a radiation transport code, not a criticality code. Specialized criticality codes (MCNP-K, KENO, Serpent) use k-eigenvalue iteration and fission-source convergence algorithms 
  that Geant4 does not implement. Geant4 just does analog Monte Carlo: it tracks every secondary neutron from every fission until it escapes, gets captured, or falls below threshold.
                                                                                                                                                                                                        
  Two practical consequences if you make the target large enough that secondaries cause more fissions:                                                                                                  
   
  - Performance. Each prompt fission neutron that fissions again produces ~2.4 more neutrons. If your geometry has k_eff close to 1, a single primary can trigger thousands of secondary fissions within
   one event — runtime per event blows up, memory blows up, the OGL viewer chokes on track storage. With k_eff > 1, runtime per event is unbounded.
  - Validity. Geant4 wasn't validated for this regime. Subtle issues (delayed-neutron treatment, fission spectrum sampling, multi-generation tally bias) that don't matter for sub-mfp foils start      
  mattering. Results may look reasonable and be wrong.                                                                                                                                                  
   
  3. Where the safe zone is                                                                                                                                                                             
                  
  - Sub-cm cubes (e.g. 5 mm cube, 5 × 5 × 1 mm slab): mass ~tens to hundreds of mg, k_eff effectively 0, every primary causes at most ~1 fission, secondaries leak. This is the regime where Geant4 is  
  doing what it's good at.
  - ~1 cm cube (~19 g): still very subcritical, but you'll start seeing the occasional secondary fission inside the bulk. Still fine, just not single-interaction-clean anymore.                        
  - Several cm cubes: starting to enter "you're modeling a small fission assembly" territory — runtime grows, results need to be interpreted carefully, and you're past what this code was set up to do.
                                                                                                                                                                                                        
  So the practical recommendation: if you go to a cube, keep it ≤ a few mm on a side. That's plenty of mfp for ~100% interaction probability per primary, but small enough that secondary neutrons leak 
  instead of multiplying, which keeps each event clean and Geant4 in its validated regime.

  UIUC — inherits from FTFP_BERT                                                                                                                                                                        
                                                                                                                                                                                                        
  class MyPhysicsList : public FTFP_BERT { ... }
                                                                                                                                                                                                        
  FTFP_BERT is a Geant4 reference physics list — a pre-assembled bundle that already includes EM, hadronic, decay, and ion physics internally. By inheriting from it, the UIUC sim gets all of that for 
  free before the constructor even runs. The three RegisterPhysics calls in Physics.cc then add on top of that inherited baseline (EM standard, optical, decay — most of which FTFP_BERT already        
  provides, so there's some redundancy there).                                                                                                                                                          
                                                                                                                                                                                                        
  The split between .hh (declaration only: MyPhysicsList();) and .cc (definition) is the conventional C++ pattern — the class is declared in the header, the constructor body lives in the .cc.         
                                                                                                                                                                                                        
  ---                                                                                                                                                                                                   
  Nuclear fission sim — inherits from G4VModularPhysicsList                                                                                                                                           
                                                                                                                                                                                                        
  class MyPhysicsList : public G4VModularPhysicsList { ... }
                                                                                                                                                                                                        
  G4VModularPhysicsList is the bare base class — it does nothing on its own. Every process must be registered explicitly. The constructor in Physics.hh hand-picks exactly the modules needed: HP       
  neutron transport (QGSP_BIC_HP), hadronic elastic HP, radioactive decay, ion physics, etc. This gives you precise control, which matters here because you specifically need the High Precision (HP)
  neutron data library for the U-235 fission cross-sections below 20 MeV — FTFP_BERT uses BERT cascade which doesn't give you that.                                                                     
                  
  The .cc being just #include "Physics.hh" is a consequence of the inline constructor: since the constructor body is written inside the class definition in the header, there's nothing left to define  
  in the .cc.
                                                                                                                                                                                                        
  ---             
  Key practical difference: FTFP_BERT is a convenient turnkey list suited to the UIUC muography use case (muons through rock/concrete — no need for thermal neutron cross-sections). The fission sim
  needs the HP neutron stack, so it builds from scratch with G4VModularPhysicsList and explicitly registers QGSP_BIC_HP + HadronElasticPhysicsHP. You can't easily get that out of FTFP_BERT.

  The fission sim is clean.   FTFP_BERT already internally registers EM physics and decay physics. Then the UIUC constructor does this:                                                                                             
                                                                                                                                                                                                        
  RegisterPhysics(new G4EmStandardPhysics());   // FTFP_BERT already has this                                                                                                                           
  RegisterPhysics(new G4OpticalPhysics());       // fine — FTFP_BERT doesn't include this                                                                                                               
  RegisterPhysics(new G4DecayPhysics());         // FTFP_BERT already has this                                                                                                                          
                                                                                                                                                                                                        
  When you register a physics module whose name collides with one already registered, Geant4 prints a warning and deletes the old one, replacing it. So the UIUC sim is silently downgrading its own EM

  material it probably doesn't matter much in practice, but it's unintentional.                                                                                                                         
                  
  The only registration in that constructor that's actually doing useful work is G4OpticalPhysics() — that's the one thing FTFP_BERT doesn't bring. The other two calls are redundant at best, a silent 
  downgrade at worst.
                                                                                                                                                                                                        
  The fission sim avoids this entirely by starting from G4VModularPhysicsList and being explicit about everything it registers — no hidden inherited state to conflict with.

Let me lay out the full bookkeeping.                                                                                                                                  
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  The full energy balance, all-in-one                                                                                                                                                                     
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  Consider the entire reaction from initial state to fully-stable end state:                                                                                                                              
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  n + U-235  →  (everything stable, plus all photons and neutrinos ever emitted)                                                                                                                          
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  The mass of the left side exceeds the mass of the right side by ~200 MeV/c². That ~200 MeV is the total Q-value to stability — every joule of energy that this reaction will ever release. Energy       
  conservation says this 200 MeV must show up as kinetic energy + photon energy + neutrino energy of the products. Always. No exceptions.                                                                 
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  But the mass-to-energy conversion doesn't all happen at once. It's released in stages.                                                                                                                  
  
  Stage 1: prompt fission (~182 MeV released)                                                                                                                                                             
                                                        
  n + U-235  →  Xe137* + Sr97* + 3n + 7γ                                                                                                                                                                  
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  (starred = unstable). Mass balance:                                                                                                                                                                     
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  m(n) + m(U-235)  −  [m(Xe137*) + m(Sr97*) + 3·m(n)]  ≈  182 MeV / c²                                                                                                                                    
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  So at the moment of fission, ~182 MeV of rest mass disappears, reappearing as:                                                                                                                          
  - ~165 MeV fragment KE                                                                                                                                                                                  
  - ~5 MeV prompt-neutron KE                                                                                                                                                                              
  - ~7 MeV prompt-gamma energy                          
  - (small mass change from neutron count, etc.)
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  Notice the unstable fragments still carry rest mass that's heavier than what they will eventually become. That rest-mass excess is energy that's "stored" in the fragments, just not yet released.
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  Stage 2: decay chain (~18 MeV released, in pieces)                                                                                                                                                      
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  Now Xe137* beta-decays:                                                                                                                                                                                 
                                                        
  Xe137*  →  Cs137* + e⁻ + ν̄_e
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  Mass balance for this step:
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  m(Xe137*)  −  [m(Cs137*) + m(e⁻)]  ≈  4.16 MeV / c²   
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  So another 4.16 MeV of rest mass disappears, reappearing as:                                                                                                                                            
  - ~1.4 MeV β KE (mean of the continuous spectrum)                                                                                                                                                       
  - ~2.7 MeV ν̄ KE                                                                                                                                                                                         
  - ~0.07 MeV Cs137 recoil                              
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  This 4.16 MeV did not come from the fragment's earlier 70 MeV kinetic energy — that 70 MeV had already been spent slowing the fragment down by ionizing the foil (heat + delta electrons). The 4.16 MeV 
  is new energy, freshly converted from Xe137's rest-mass excess into kinetic energy of its decay products.                                                                                               
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  Then Cs137* may decay further (Cs137 → Ba137 + e⁻ + ν̄ + γ), releasing another ~1 MeV. And so on, until you reach a stable nucleus. Add up all those decay-step Q-values along both fragments' chains and
   you get the missing ~18 MeV.                         
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  So the total = prompt + chain                         

  total Q to stability  =  Q_prompt          +  Σ Q_decay_step
                        ≈  182 MeV           +  18 MeV                                                                                                                                                    
                        ≈  200 MeV                                                                                                                                                                        
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  Both terms are real, distinct mass-to-energy conversion events happening at different points in time. Both are accounted for in the truth-record (each particle's initial_KE_MeV is its KE at the moment
   of birth). Summing all those KEs across the event gives ~200 MeV — the full Q-value — which equals the rest-mass deficit between the initial state and the stable end state.

⏺ Honest answer: there isn't a standard nuclear-physics Sankey for fission decay chains — and it's exactly because of the conservation-vs-clarity tension we keep hitting. What's standard in the field is
   two distinct diagram types, used in parallel, each doing one job well:
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  Standard #1: Static energy-partition diagram (for the budget)                                                                                                                                           
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  The textbook fission energy budget is always drawn as a pie chart or stacked bar with ~6 wedges:                                                                                                        
                                                        
  ┌────────────────────┬───────┐                                                                                                                                                                          
  │      Channel       │  MeV  │                        
  ├────────────────────┼───────┤                                                                                                                                                                          
  │ Fragment KE        │ 169.1 │
  ├────────────────────┼───────┤                                                                                                                                                                          
  │ Prompt neutron KE  │ 4.8   │                        
  ├────────────────────┼───────┤                                                                                                                                                                          
  │ Prompt γ           │ 7.0   │
  ├────────────────────┼───────┤                                                                                                                                                                          
  │ β KE (decay chain) │ 7.0   │                        
  ├────────────────────┼───────┤                                                                                                                                                                          
  │ β-delayed γ        │ 6.3   │
  ├────────────────────┼───────┤                                                                                                                                                                          
  │ Anti-νₑ (escape)   │ 8.7   │                        
  ├────────────────────┼───────┤                                                                                                                                                                          
  │ Total              │ 202.9 │                        
  └────────────────────┴───────┘                                                                                                                                                                          
  
  (Numbers from JEFF-3.3 / ENDF/B-VIII.0 for thermal U-235.) No topology, no chronology, no parent-child information — just where the 200 MeV ends up. Conservation is trivial because it's a partition:  
  the wedges sum to the Q-value by construction. This is the diagram you see in Lamarsh, Glasstone & Sesonske, and every reactor-physics textbook. It's the answer to "where does the energy go?" and it's
   been the standard for ~50 years.                                                                                                                                                                       
                                                        
  Standard #2: Nuclear level scheme / decay scheme (for topology + conservation)                                                                                                                          
  
  For showing how energy is released along a decay chain, the standard is the level scheme (also called decay scheme). You see these in NNDC's ENSDF database, the Karlsruhe Nuklidkarte, and in any      
  nuclear-data reference.                               
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  Layout:                                               

  - Vertical axis: mass excess of the system (or equivalently, energy above the stable end-state).                                                                                                        
  - Each nuclide is a horizontal line at its mass-excess energy.
  - Each decay step is an arrow from a higher level to a lower level, labeled with the Q-value of the step plus what's emitted (β⁻ + ν̄ + γ_E1 + γ_E2 + …) and the branching ratio.                        
  - The vertical drop between two consecutive levels equals exactly the Q-value of the decay → conservation is enforced visually as "energy decreases monotonically toward the stable ground state."      
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  For Xe137 → Cs137 → Ba137*(stable) you'd see:                                                                                                                                                           
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  Xe137*  ── E_excess = +5.1 MeV  ←───── start (β⁻ decay, Q = 4.16 MeV)                                                                                                                                   
                       │                                                                                                                                                                                  
                       ↓  emits e⁻ (~1.4 MeV mean) + ν̄ (~2.7 MeV) + γ_661.7
  Cs137*  ── E_excess = +0.94 MeV (β⁻ decay, Q = 1.18 MeV, t½ = 30 yr)                                                                                                                                    
                       │                                                                                                                                                                                  
                       ↓  emits e⁻ + ν̄ + γ_283.5                                                                                                                                                          
  Ba137   ── E_excess = 0.0 MeV  ←───── stable                                                                                                                                                            
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  These respect conservation exactly because they're energy-level diagrams: vertical position is energy, vertical drops are Q-values, and the diagram makes the "amount of remaining mass-energy to be    
  released" visible as the height above the stable line. They also handle multiple emitted particles per step naturally (one arrow per emission, sub-labeled with energy).                                
                                                                                                                                                                                                          
  What level schemes don't do well: aggregate budget (you can't read "fragment KE = 169 MeV" off them), and parallel chains from two independent fragments (typically drawn as separate diagrams).        
  
  The third path nobody really uses                                                                                                                                                                       
                                                        
  Strict-conservation Sankeys of decay chains do appear, but rarely, and almost always with an explicit "heat dissipation" or "non-radiative" sink to absorb the parent's leftover KE — exactly option (c)
   from earlier. You see this occasionally in reactor-physics calorimetric diagrams: "of the 200 MeV per fission, 90 % stays in the fuel as heat, 5 % deposits in moderator, 4 % escapes as neutrinos,
  ..." That kind of diagram is a fate Sankey (where each MeV ends up depositing), not a decay-tree Sankey (parent → child topology). It conserves trivially because it tracks "deposition location" rather
   than "particle identity."
